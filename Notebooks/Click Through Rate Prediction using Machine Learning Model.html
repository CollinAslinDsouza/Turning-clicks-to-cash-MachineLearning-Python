##KNN MODEL
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.neighbors import KNeighborsClassifier
from sklearn.metrics import classification_report

# Load the dataset
df1 = pd.read_csv("advertising.csv")

# 1. Define Features (X) and Target (y)
feature_cols = [
    'Daily Time Spent on Site',
    'Age',
    'Area Income',
    'Daily Internet Usage',
    'Male'
]
target_col = 'Clicked on Ad'

X1 = df1[feature_cols]
y1 = df1[target_col]

# 2. Split the data into training and testing sets (80% train, 20% test)
X1_train, X1_test, y1_train, y1_test = train_test_split(
    X1, y1, test_size=0.2, random_state=42
)

# 3. Standardize the data 
scaler = StandardScaler()
X1_train_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X1_train)
X1_test_scaled = scaler.transform(X1_test)

# 4. Initialize and train the K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Classifier
# n_neighbors=5 is a common choice for k
knn_model = KNeighborsClassifier(n_neighbors=5)
knn_model.fit(X1_train_scaled, y1_train)

# 5. Make predictions and Evaluate the model
y1_pred = knn_model.predict(X1_test_scaled)
report = classification_report(y1_test, y1_pred)

print(report)

The KNN model¶

The KNN Model achieves a strong overall accuracy of 93 on the test set. It exhibits a slight preference for classifying the "No Click" group (Class 0) correctly, evidenced by its higher recall (0.96), while maintaining a high precision (0.96) for the "Click" group (Class 1).

🌳 Why Random Forest is Optimal for Business Use¶

Choosing Random Forest over Gradient Boosting is often the optimal business decision due to its excellent balance of high performance and resource efficiency. It allows a business to gain maximum predictive benefit while utilizing minimum computational resources.

Here are the key points on why Random Forest (RF) is resource-efficient and highly practical for deployment:

Parallelization of Training:¶

Random Forest Advantage: Random Forest is an embarrassingly parallel algorithm. Each individual decision tree is trained independently and simultaneously , which significantly speeds up the training process, especially on modern systems with multiple cores or distributed computing resources.

GB Disadvantage: Gradient Boosting (GB) is sequential; each new tree corrects the errors of the previous one, meaning trees cannot be trained at the same time, making the training process much slower.

Reduced Overfitting and Stability:¶

Random Forest Advantage: Random Forest builds trees from bootstrapped samples of the data and uses a random subset of features for each split. This inherent randomness makes the model highly robust, less prone to overfitting, and ensures good generalization to new data without extensive tuning.

Business Benefit: A stable model requires less maintenance and fewer retraining cycles, which translates directly into lower operational costs and engineering hours.

Faster Prediction/Inference Speed:¶

Random Forest Advantage: Once trained, Random Forest models can provide predictions very quickly. The prediction process is simpler than in GB because it just averages the output of many independent trees.

Business Benefit: Faster prediction times are crucial for a dynamic advertising environment (like real-time bidding or page load), ensuring minimal latency when deciding whether to show an ad.

Fewer Hyperparameter Needs:¶

Random Forest Advantage: Random Forest typically requires tuning only a few core hyperparameters (like the number of trees and the maximum features per split) to achieve high performance.

Business Benefit: This simplicity means less time and computational power are spent on extensive grid search or complex optimization, reducing the time-to-deployment.

In summary, while Gradient Boosting often achieves marginally better peak performance, Random Forest delivers near-identical top-tier accuracy (93%) with lower computational costs, faster deployment, and superior stability, making it the most robust and financially sound choice.
